Multiplayer

Multiple users, multiple cursors, and multiple devices — one document. A single source of truth for your data, mirrored on every client.

Offline

Full functionality when offline. Your changes queue locally, and everything syncs when you reconnect.

Consistent

When users make overlapping edits, Automerge performs a consistent merge that prevents most conflicts, and gives you tools to control the outcome.

Versioned

Automerge remembers every change. Branch fearlessly, experiment boldly. Your document's entire history lives locally, ready when you need it.

Compact

Automerge supports millions of changes in a single doc. It uses a compressed columnar store on disk and in memory.

Fast

Stop waiting for light speed. If the data is already on your computer, there's literally no way to get it faster than that.

Automerge works with your existing stack.
Drop it in where you need it.

Compatible

Works with React, Svelte, and other frameworks out of the box. Plugins for tldraw, codemirror, godot, and so many more.

Network

Peer-to-peer, client-server, files on disk, email attachments… if you can transfer bytes, you can sync documents.

Language

Built for Javascript and Rust, with ports or bindings for Swift, Python, C, and more.

Backend

Works with whatever backend you already have. Or, use Automerge Repo, which comes with a sync server backend out-of-the-box. No proprietary infrastructure — you own your stack.

Built with diligence.

Backed by a decade of research, engineered for speed and correctness.

Created at Ink & Switch by the founders of Heroku and proven through academic rigor at Cambridge University, Automerge represents ten years of research and academic promise into software that handles millions of operations, runs everywhere, and never loses your work.

Full-time contributors

Alex Good and Orion Henry maintain Automerge full-time, with Ink & Switch lab's commitment to software that lasts.

Community-powered

Automerge thrives through contributions from Snap (C bindings), Zed (Go implementation), and developers worldwide who believe collaboration should be foundational, not an afterthought.
